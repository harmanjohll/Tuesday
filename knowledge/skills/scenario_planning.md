# Scenario Planning -- Strange's Playbook

## Framework: The 14 Million Futures Method

When Harman faces a strategic decision, map it systematically:

### Step 1: Define the Decision Space
- What exactly is being decided?
- What are the constraints (MOE policy, budget, timeline, staffing)?
- Who are the stakeholders and what do they care about?

### Step 2: Generate Scenarios (minimum 3)
- **Status quo** -- what happens if we do nothing?
- **Bold move** -- what's the ambitious option?
- **Middle path** -- what's the pragmatic compromise?
- For each: map first-order effects, then second-order effects

### Step 3: Stress Test Each Scenario
- What's the worst case?
- What assumption are we making that could be wrong?
- How reversible is this if it fails?
- What's the cost of being wrong vs. the cost of inaction?

### Step 4: Recommend with Trade-offs
- Present options as a decision matrix, not a single recommendation
- Be explicit about trade-offs ("You gain X but lose Y")
- Flag the scenario with the best risk-adjusted outcome
- Include the "and if everything goes wrong" contingency

## Singapore Education Context
- MOE policy cycles and how they constrain school-level decisions
- Cluster dynamics -- what the cluster superintendent cares about
- Parent expectations in Singapore (academic results, holistic development, CCA)
- Staff deployment constraints (teaching hours, subject combinations, seniority)
- Budget cycles and how to time proposals

## Decision Frameworks Harman Uses
- **Reversibility test**: Can we undo this if it doesn't work? If yes, bias toward action.
- **10/10/10 rule**: How will we feel about this in 10 minutes, 10 months, 10 years?
- **Regret minimization**: Which option would I regret NOT trying?
- **Second-order thinking**: What happens after what happens?
