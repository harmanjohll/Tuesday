## MANDATORY: Match This Voice

These are Harman's actual words. Every speech, presentation, or formal document you generate must sound like it was written by the same person. Specifically:
- Match the sentence rhythm (short for punch, long for building momentum)
- Use first person naturally -- "I", "we", "our"
- Stories and real examples before theory -- always ground ideas in lived experience
- Acronym frameworks when structuring major pieces (VOICE, BEATTY, STAR, DREAM)
- Emotional pivot near the end -- shift from intellectual to personal
- Close with call to action or memorable final line
- Reference school-specific context (Beatty, students, staff, Ohana, Ubuntu) when relevant
- Weave student names, real questions, and specific achievements into the narrative
- Balance formality with warmth -- dignified but never cold

If the output doesn't sound like these exemplars, it's wrong. Revise.

## Exemplar 1: Annual Awards Day 2025 (VOICE Framework)

*Context: Formal ceremony speech for Beatty Secondary School's 72nd Annual Awards Day. Guest-of-honour present. Audience: parents, staff, students, alumni, SAC members. Theme: VOICE (Values, Ownership, Inclusion, Contribution, Empowerment).*

Dr Maung, Guest-of-Honour and esteemed alumnus of Beatty Secondary,

Mrs Tan Wie Pin, Superintendent South 3 Cluster,

Distinguished members of the School Advisory Committee, Beatty Alumni, and Parent Support Group,

Dear parents, partners, colleagues, and of course, my wonderful students, 

Good evening and welcome to Beatty Secondary School’s 72nd Annual Awards Day.

Let’s begin with the why behind our theme. Even more poignant: why have a theme at all, really? To me, themes are not simplistically about some seasonal flavour that ebbs and tides; it is a means to deepen understanding and build culture by unveiling what we value and how we go about demonstrating those values. So why choose the theme of celebrating voices and building our future together? Allow me to take you through that. 

Over the past week, I had asked our incumbent and incoming Student Council EXCO to ask some questions about matters that are important to them. I begin this evening with four of those questions.

The first two are similar: “What would the working world be like in the future?” “What would my future career path look like?”

Next: “Given the political state of the world, what is the most concerning thing for schools?”

Finally: “How might the uprising role of AI shape the future of education and the job market?”

These questions, asked by Yselle, Alyssa, Matthew and Vera, are not just reflective, but urgent. They reflect where we are and hint at what might lie ahead. They frame the world our students are stepping into, one that is uncertain, fast-changing, and deeply complex. The challenge of this complicated, complex, perhaps even chaotic reality is in figuring out what to do. Far too often, we know what the answers might be — especially on a test (okay, maybe not all the time, but you get the idea). It is a stark reminder that we must look beyond metrics that we are familiar with in order to navigate chaos with clarity, difference with dignity, and our future with fortitude.

VOICE is fundamental to thriving in a world that is fractured, fragmented, fractioned and at times, fraught. And let me be clear: it is not voice for voice’s sake. Voice must come with the deep yoke of respect and responsibility. Voice that is value-less is simply noise. Voice that is rooted in values, espouses ownership, enables inclusion, exemplifies contribution and encourages empowerment will allow for us to build our individual and collective futures together. These tie with questions from Brenda, Yoong Le and Lucas: about how we ensure harmony and stability as we navigate uncertainty and address issues that are of local and global concern. But voice must be more than presence. It must be principled.

While we notice shifts, tectonic or otherwise, in the world within and around us, we must maintain hope. Voice must resist alarmism and blind optimism. It must reflect hope with clarity. We can be mindful and watchful, and at the same time, maintain hope in turning challenge into opportunities; be tempered and measured. So we return to education, our very essence and purpose of all that we do here. How might education shift? Do we learn only to gain mastery, or also to innovate? To only thrive in industry, or to understand ourselves better? Is it about complete control, or might we learn what exercising compassion is really about? To me, and not to sound overtly greedy: it’s all of these. And the value lies not in choosing one, but in how they intersect, overlap, and shape us.

Measures and metrics are important. How we go about doing this is perhaps even more critical. What we measure somehow pays sometimes disproportionate attention to that item or ideal. That’s why we have refreshed our school awards. Not to just reward outcomes, but to recognise growth, not arrival; excellence, not perfection; capacity and competencies, not just capability and grades; endeavour and enterprise, not just participation. You will hear more about these awards during the ceremony. For now, I would like to take a little time to express my deep gratitude to my colleagues who have spent much time thinking through these awards, the SAC and Alumni for supporting us in this endeavour, and, of course, our many generous and ardent donors and sponsors, both long-standing and new, whose belief in nurturing the whole learner to learn, live and love. 

We remember the late Ms Renuka d/o Sathianathan, an extraordinary educator whose philosophy of serving more than achieving continues through the Humanities Voyager award, a tribute to empathy, critical thinking and global literacy. Ms Rajeshwari Rai, whose own journey in the arts began right here at Beatty, whose Creative Excellence Award tells every young learner and artist: “I see you. I see your potential.” And we are grateful to the Hee family who have reinstated a reimagined version of the previous award, the STEM Excellence Award, in memory of Mr Hee Swee Khee, whose legacy reflects compassion and a belief in the transcendence of education. 

I am also grateful to our Alumni, who have also sponsored a new award, the Bilingual Bridge Award, to celebrate excellence in the English Language and Mother Tongue Languages. In the space of sporting excellence, I’m grateful to Mr Loganathan for continuing to support us in recognising the passion and achievements of our athletes. 

Mdm Yeong, thank you for continuing to support us in valuing service and contribution through your CARE award. And of course, Mr Low Cheng Lum, in addition to the many awards already sponsored by him, for supporting us in another new award for excellence in Civic, Cultural and Global Literacy, a means to recognise the intangible and helping us pull together achievement, acknowledgment and actualisation when it comes to our NEXUS@BTY Programme.  

Our students have done well this year across all domains. Our Uniformed Groups attained distinction across the board. Our Football B boys are now in the semi-finals of Tier 3. And fresh off the press would be our performance at the recently-concluded Canoeing NSG where our canoeists have done really well, securing strong finishes in various K4 and K2 events. Our Concert Band, Chinese Orchestra and Choir have attained accomplishments at the SYF (and what stood out for me when I watched two of the performances is how they have improved and grown over the last two to three years; that is perhaps more important to me). Also fresh off the press, our Malay Dance group have been accorded a Distinction at their SYF! (Please join me in congratulating all of the students, teachers and instructors). We continue to deliver value-added performance in national examinations. They have achieved with heart. They have led with humility. They have made us proud of the spirit with which they connect, create, and contribute. 

What then of things that go beyond? Another Student Councillor asked, “What subjects might we teach more of in school?” I can interpret that as how might we learn differently. We have, and will continue, to broaden our experiences in school, including looking beyond metrics and measures we are familiar with. Since 2024, we have embarked on broadening our partnerships. We started with A*STAR, Pacific International Lines and Rockwell Automation, our stalwart industry partners now, and of course, our partner schools in Japan. Since then, we have added exchanges to Korea, Bali, and now Estonia (one this year, and another next year – for the aesthetics). In the space of industry, we have teamed up with ITE (West) to afford our students micro credentials while working with MNCs, with the vernacular news teams at SPH, and from next year, we may add ST Engineering. To the students, please join me in thanking your dedicated teachers who have emotionally invested above and beyond to secure these partnerships and to guide you as mentors as you work with our partners at these various institutions. 

What else can we do to inspire and invigorate learning? If you look around, our school has and is going through several renewal projects. The Secondary Ones are already experiencing our refreshed classrooms – new furniture, new feel, but most importantly, new ways of thinking about space and learning. We had originally planned to space the development of classrooms across four or five years. Thanks to Mr Low Cheng Lum, again, we are now able to accelerate this to within two years. This means that all classrooms, by the end of 2026, would be completely refitted with new facilities. Mr Low, who is unable to join us today, is a proud alumnus of Beatty Secondary, and has been supporting us since the 1990s, through facility development, in sponsoring many awards and scholarships for the students, and in taking care of teachers, too! Mr Low’s belief in our learners and teachers is testament to what it means to contribute and build our future together. My deepest gratitude to Mr Low for investing in our future and in our people. 

So where do we go from here? Sathika asks, “What should students really be concerned about?”

I say: let’s start with the things that matter. When we speak about voice, we are speaking about Values, Ownership, Inclusion, Contribution and Empowerment. I invite all of us to think about what these are really about, and how they impact how we are being and becoming, as individuals and as communities. 

Let’s raise our VOICE, not just in volume, but in values, grounded in respect, rights, and responsibility. Guided by agency, accountability, and awareness. Let’s stand for something, not just speak about everything.

Beattyians, your job is not just to achieve. It is to become, and in becoming, to inspire. To grow into adaptive thinkers, agile learners, and active contributors. To be purpose-driven stewards who shape systems, not just survive within them.

So, I leave you with this:

Be courageous in complexity. Be humble in achievement. Be generous in contribution.

When we are together as an Ohana, we will build an even better tomorrow 

Thank you, and once again, congratulations to all our award recipients. Non Vi Sed Arte!

## Exemplar 2: Gala Dinner Speech (BEATTY Framework)

*Context: 70th anniversary gala dinner for Beatty Secondary School + 30th anniversary of the Beatty Alumni. Minister of State present. Audience: alumni spanning decades, community leaders, staff. Framework: BEATTY (Build lives, Empower, Adaptability, Tenacity, Trailblazing, Yield excellence).*

Ms Gan Siow Huang, Minister of State for Education & Minister of State for Manpower ;

Mr Andrew Lim, Zonal Director Schools, South;

Mrs Low Chew Lian, Superintendent, South 3 Cluster;

Mr Low Cheng Lum, Distinguished Alumni and First Patron of Beatty Alumni;

Ms Janet Yeong, Patron of the Beatty Alumni

Mrs Angeline Goh, spouse of our beloved Mr Goh Joon Seng;

Mr Bob Fang, Distinguished Alumni and First President of the Beatty Alumni;

Mr Jimmy Soon, Chairman, School Advisory Committee;

Mr Patrick Lim, President, Beatty Alumni;

Distinguished ex-Principals, Vice-Principals, HODs and teachers;

Our supportive parents & partners;

Beattyians, past & present, colleagues, students, ladies and gentlemen. 

Tonight, we celebrate two milestones: Our 70th anniversary as a school, and the 30th anniversary of our alumni. These dual milestones thread many generations of Beattyians together, an extended Ohana that thrives in the spirit of Ubuntu – I am because we are. Tonight, I’d thought it would be useful to consider our odyssey as a port-of-call, to reflect on the narrative that is Beatty, to illuminate our legacy, incandesce our present zeitgeist and think about how we might ignite our vision. 

For seven decades, Beatty stands out when it comes to building lives. This of course is the foremost purpose of education: to mould minds for the collective good. For us, this is more than a singular mission; it is a beacon of light and hope to many Beattyians. To us, since days past, today, and in future, education transcends academia. It is about moulding character, imbibing and having a strong sense of values, and preparing students for life. It is about enabling all within the Ohana to learn to learn, learn to live and to learn to love. When we do these well, all else will fall in place. To actualise these requires several key dispositions, dispositions not just for students, but also for teachers, alumni, parents and others in our community. 

First, we must remain committed to empowering all in our community with opportunities to hone their growth and self-mastery. This is key to nurturing adaptive thinkers, agile learners, and active contributors. With each passing decade, the tides of change have reshaped our education system, but the beacon of empowerment remains constant. This dedication to adaptability is no stranger to us; it's a tried-and-true companion that has stood with us through changes as dramatic as shifts in the student body and as practical as the design of our learning spaces.

Second, it points to adaptability and adaptive thinking. Tides and phases of change is what keeps life interesting: it enables inquiry, nurtures us through its intensity, and provides a sense of intentionality. Adaptability is critical to ensure that we uphold our fundamental principles and values while meeting each individual at where they are at. And this is what we are about: being inclusive and affording opportunities for all to develop their sense of adapting and embracing the world around them. 

Third, we must know that this comes hand-in-hand with a sense of tenacity: that gumption and grit that our Ohana has shown through the years. Our journey, both as individuals and as a collective, has certainly be perfectly imperfect, filled with trials and triumphs. But we must always keep a basic tenet in mind: the challenges presented are opportunities for us to sharpen our minds and soften our hearts. When we think further, tenacity might point a little more to a communal value, rather than something we associate simply with an individual. This again speaks to the distinctive nature of our Ohana, and how we can thrive – and be tenacious – in the spirit of Ubuntu, where I am because we are. 

With these, we can be confident of a community that is trailblazing and one that yields excellence, not just in the context of personal accolades, but by how much they contribute and uplift others. This is what it means to forge frontiers and is what is most crucial and matters most, especially in this day and age. We must return, revisit and reinvigorate the elements of Ohana and Ubuntu – I am because we are – in order to both thrive and flourish, both individually and collectively. Amidst us this evening, there are many Beattyians who are testament to these capacities. I would like to pay tribute to the pioneers of our story - the very first cohort of Beattyians. It is in their footsteps that we continue to tread, and their legacy that we honour as we write our chapters. Among them is Mr Bob Fang, who given his trailblazing, enterprising and altruistic nature, founded the Beatty Alumni, which is 30 years old today. His actions have paved the way for strong ties and partnership that I am confident will continue. Yielding excellence and being trailblazing is about how we contribute to society and the legacy we leave behind. We envisage Beattyians as passionate and purposeful leaders, each charting their unique course and bound by the shared ethos of BEATTY.

As we culminate our year-long 70th-anniversary celebrations, we are reminded of our duty towards nurturing the future of our school, safeguarding its values, and honouring its history. Our 70-year odyssey is but a port-of-call, a point for introspection. Beatty, like a sturdy ship, has gotten this far because of our remarkable crew over the many years, partners, educators, alumni and students who are all emotionally invested in ensuring the very best for those around them. 

I would like to take some time to thank everyone who has contributed to this remarkable voyage and to the success that is this evening. Ms Gan, thank you for joining us on this momentous occasion. Mr Low Cheng Lum, for supporting teachers and students in so many different ways through the years, and again this evening with this dinner. Beatty thrives better because of your contribution to our community, and we will always be grateful for all that you have done for your alma mater over the many years. To my amazing and wonderful colleagues and students, your indomitable spirit shines through all the elements of this evening's event. The Coffee Table Book is absolute class, and I'm certain everyone here tonight agrees with me. The design of the tote bags and the knitting of the bees were tasks that seemed insurmountable at first, but your passion and resolve overcame those challenges. Thank you for pulling together all the production elements for this evening. Every piece contributes to making this a memorable celebration. And this is on top of weaving elements into our curriculum to both ensure that the celebrations were for everyone in the school community and also to introduce some models of curriculum that would also steer us forward. I am truly humbled to work alongside all of you, and I am confident that we will be able to actualise wonderful outcomes in the years ahead. To Ms Janet Yeong, Mr Jimmy Soon, Mrs Low Chew Lian and Mr Andrew Lim, I thank you for the support and advice that you have given me and the school; your advice has always served us well. Finally, I thank Patrick, President of the Beatty Alumni, and his team, for working closely with us to put the various segments and elements of our celebrations together. Through it all, you were patient and ensured that we were able to do justice to this significant event and milestone. And of course, a big thank you to everyone who made time to join us this evening! I trust you will enjoy the camaraderie and fellowship. 

As we chart our course forward, let us remember that each one of us is part of the Beatty story, a sparkling node like what you see on the coffee-table book. We all hold the responsibility and the honour of shaping this narrative, carrying the spirit of BEATTY within us – to build lives by empowering others to become adaptable and tenacious, and to forge frontiers in uplifting others by being trailblazing and yielding excellence. 

To 70 years of shaping minds, touching hearts, and inspiring lives; and for the Beatty Alumni - to 30 years of unity and fellowship; and to a shared future filled with learning & growth. 

Non Vi Sed Arte. Thank you.

## Exemplar 3: Graduation Speech 2024

*Context: Sec 4 graduation ceremony. Audience: graduating students, parents, teachers. Structure: 3-part advice format (Find Strength Through Values, Develop Potential from Within, Enable Realisation with Community). Personal, advisory tone.*

2024 Sec 4 Graduation Speech

Introduction 

Today, we gather to celebrate a milestone in your lives—a moment that marks both an end and a beginning. As I look out at all of you, I see not just graduates but a resilient cohort who has navigated unprecedented times and is now poised to discover their dreams. 

In my final formal speech to you – since there’s a little address that I’ll make during Prom – I have three pieces of advice as you commence your journey beyond Beatty Secondary. 

Find Strength Through Values

Success doesn't come from wishful thinking but from consistent effort. To be great at anything that you do, be disciplined. Your routines and habits determine your success. So, develop habits that align with your aspirations. Instant gratification never works; if anything, it is just a way of lying to yourself. Success is a journey; take it one step at a time. And along the way, you’ll discover more about yourself that you already know. 

Let’s go back to 2020, when you were taking your PSLE amidst a global pandemic. Uncertainty was your constant companion, yet you persevered. You adapted to virtual classrooms, missed out on gatherings, and learned to connect in new ways. This experience didn't just test you; it forged you into individuals who are more resilient and adaptable than you might realise.

Beyond that difficult year, there were indeed other challenges, setbacks, and moments in secondary school when things did not go as planned. Yet, it is in these very moments that true growth happens. Reflecting on your time here, you might recall lessons from classrooms, laughter shared along corridors, and even the challenges that seemed insurmountable at the time. But remember, 'Failure and setbacks help us improve.' They are not the end but rather stepping stones to something greater.

The cliché is that the only constant in life is change. As you've already shown by navigating the uncertainties of the past years, you have the capacity to adjust and thrive in new environments. Life will knock you down, but it's how you get back up that matters. 'If you get tired, learn to rest, not quit.' Embrace challenges as opportunities to grow, not obstacles to deter you.

In a world that is increasingly connected yet divided, empathy will be your superpower. Understand others, listen more than you speak, and you'll find that relationships become richer and more meaningful.

Be aware. 'You have power over your mind, not outside events. Realise this, and you will find strength.' Be aware of your thoughts and actions, and never underestimate the power of what you tell yourself. Sometimes, it is the simple and little things that make the most impact. 

Develop Potential from Within

As you move forward, I urge you to navigate your own narratives. Don't let others write your story. Surround yourself with people who uplift you, who challenge you to be better. Be friends with people who want the best for you.' Your inner circle can be your greatest asset or your biggest limitation—choose wisely. The best example of this would be to see how much you have accomplished in your CCAs. Would you have even imagined going that far when you first began? It is the group that you are with that allows you to go far. 

Allow yourself to aspire. 'How long will you wait before you demand the best for and of yourself?' Dream big, but also take the steps necessary to turn those dreams into reality.

As you stand on the cusp of this new chapter, know that you already possess everything you need to succeed. You are, quite literally, made of stardust since every atom in your body was once part of a star.  The universe doesn't just surround you; it is within you. So when you face doubt, remember the cosmic wonder that resides in you.

Enable Realisation with Community

We have, over the past 4 or 5 years, done our level best to nurture you to become Adaptive Thinkers, Agile Learners, and Active Contributors. So, what next? 

‘To be truly rich, give. To succeed, serve. To be loved, love.' The greatest achievements are not those measured by personal gain but by the positive impact you have on others.

Go out into the world and become purpose-driven stewards. Carry these ideals with you. Let them guide your choices and fuel your passion to make a difference.

For now, and especially today, take a moment to reflect on how far you have come. And when think about it, this would only have been made possible because of the people around us. 'We are who we are because of those around us.' Take a moment to express gratitude to your parents who have been your unwavering support, your teachers who have been your mentors, and your friends who have been your companions on this journey. 

[Slide Change] Today marks the beginning of new adventures (after your exams, of course). Navigate your narratives with courage, discover your dreams with passion, and never stop striving to be the best version of yourself, not with force but with skill. 

Non Vi Sed Arte, and once again, Congratulations to all students, parents and teachers!
